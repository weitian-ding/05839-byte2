Is the data complete?

Is there missing data or reporting error?

Since the wine review data is scraped from a website, WineEnthusiast, there could be missing fields due to missing fields on WineEnthusiast or scrapping errors. Table 1 shows the number of NA values for the columns of interest out of 129,971 wine reviews. Since the rows with one or more missing values consist only a small fraction of the wine reviews scraped, we simply drop the rows with NA values in the following analysis.

Also, since WineEnthusiast scrapping can introduce junk values due to irregularities in the website UI, more on this in the data coherence section.

Is the data appropriate?

The wine review dataset can help to answer a number of questions. Say, we want to infer the price of wine using other features like ratings and country. Since the ratings of wine is a discrete random variable (it is always a multiple of 2.5), for each wine rating bucket, we can plot a box plot of wine price (see figure 1). In general, we see a weak linear relationship between wine rating and wine price: the higher the rating is, the more expensive the wine gets.

Another example is that we want to predict wine variety using features like wine price, producing country, ratings, and description. For top 10 most-frequently reviewed wine varieties, we plot box plots of wine price (see figure 2). We see that different wine varieties have slightly different summary statistics. For example, Cabernet Sauvignon seems to produce higher-end wine, but Chardonnary is inexpensive in general with the third quantile below $40. We can also look at how wine variety influences wine ratings (see figure 3). We see that wine ratings seem to have similar summary statistics across wine variety and thus wine rating may not be a strong indicator of wine variety.

Alternatively, we can examine how wine produced in different countries differ. Figure 4 shows that the price of wine has a different summary statistics in different countries, and figure 5 shows that the country where wine is produced does not seem to influence wine rating by too much.

We can also train predictive models using the text description in wine reviews. Using word cloud, we found that “black cherry” is one of the frequently appearing word n-gram in the wine review corpus. We can categorize wine reviews into two groups based on whether they contain the word “black cherry”, and plot boxplots of wine price and rating (see figure 6 and figure 7). We see different summary statistics in these two wine review groups which suggest the word "black cherry" has some predictive power over wine price and ratings.
